Freedom in Canada

The end of the line — how freedom seekers built new lives across the border and what their arrival meant for the meaning of liberty in North America.

Why Canada?

For freedom seekers on the Underground Railroad, reaching a free northern state was not enough. The Fugitive Slave Act of 1793, and especially its 1850 successor, meant that no Black person in the United States was entirely safe. Slave catchers operated openly in northern cities; free Black people could be seized on the claim that they were escaped slaves; northern courts and officials were required by federal law to cooperate with the machinery of recapture. The only place beyond the reach of American slave law was Canada.

Britain had abolished slavery throughout its empire in 1833. Canada West (present-day Ontario) — separated from the United States by the Great Lakes, the Niagara River, and the St. Lawrence — became the primary destination for freedom seekers following the northern routes of the Underground Railroad. Under British law, a person who reached Canadian soil was free. American authorities could not legally compel Canadian officials to return them.

"I looked at my hands to see if I was the same person now that I was free. There was such a glory over everything — the sun came out like gold through the trees, and over the fields, and I felt like I was in Heaven." — Harriet Tubman, on crossing into Canada for the first time, as recounted by Kate Clifford Larson

Black Communities in Canada West

Freedom did not mean an empty landscape. Black people who crossed into Canada found — and built — vibrant communities. Towns like Windsor, Chatham, St. Catharines, and Hamilton had significant Black populations by the 1840s and 1850s. Some of these communities had been founded specifically as destinations for freedom seekers; others had grown organically as Black people settled, found work, and created families in a place where their legal status was secure.

The church was once again central. AME and AME Zion congregations established in Canada West provided the same combination of spiritual sustenance and community organization that they had in cities like Middletown, Connecticut. Ministers who had operated as Underground Railroad conductors in the United States sometimes relocated to Canada to continue supporting their communities. The networks that had carried people northward were repurposed to help them settle and thrive.

Mutual aid societies, schools, and newspapers emerged in Black Canadian communities. The Voice of the Fugitive, edited by Henry Bibb, was published in Sandwich (now Windsor), Ontario. The Provincial Freeman, edited partly by Mary Ann Shadd Cary — one of the first Black female newspaper editors in North America — published from Toronto and Chatham. These publications connected Black Canadians to each other and to the broader abolitionist movement in the United States.

Organized Freedom: The Dawn and Buxton Settlements

Some of the most remarkable experiments in freedom occurred in planned Black settlements in Canada West. The Dawn Settlement near Dresden, Ontario was established in the 1840s under the guidance of Josiah Henson — a formerly enslaved man whose life story partly inspired Harriet Beecher Stowe's Uncle Tom's Cabin. Dawn included the British American Institute, a manual labor school intended to provide education and vocational training to freedom seekers.

The Elgin Settlement, also known as the Buxton Settlement, was founded in 1849 by Reverend William King, a white Presbyterian minister who freed the people he had inherited and then dedicated himself to establishing a community where Black settlers could own land, build homes, and educate their children. By 1860, Buxton had a population of over a thousand Black residents, a school whose graduates sometimes outperformed white students from surrounding towns, and a thriving agricultural economy.

Key Black Communities in Canada West (Ontario)

  • St. Catharines — Harriet Tubman's base of operations during her years as a conductor; home to a significant AME Zion congregation
  • Windsor / Sandwich — Major entry point across from Detroit; home of The Voice of the Fugitive newspaper
  • Chatham — Large, organized Black community; site of John Brown's 1858 convention; home of The Provincial Freeman
  • Dawn Settlement (Dresden) — Josiah Henson's community; included the British American Institute school
  • Buxton (Elgin Settlement) — Planned agricultural community known for its school and economic success
  • Hamilton — Urban center with Black neighborhoods, churches, and abolitionist connections to New England

The Line from Middletown to Canada

The freedom seekers who passed through Middletown, Connecticut were connected — through the same church networks, minister relationships, and abolitionist organizations — to the communities that awaited them in Canada. The AME Zion denomination, which grounded Jehiel Beman's work in Middletown, had congregations on both sides of the border. Ministers traveled, corresponded, and coordinated across this entire geography.

When a freedom seeker arrived in Middletown — exhausted, frightened, perhaps separated from family — they entered a network whose reach extended from the Virginia Tidewater to the Ontario lakeshore. The Beman family's abolitionist correspondence, their participation in national antislavery conventions, and their pastoral connections made them part of a continental freedom movement. Middletown was not an isolated act of local generosity; it was one node in a network whose logic and destination was freedom in its fullest form.

From Middletown, the route northward through Hartford and Springfield connected to upstate New York — Syracuse, Rochester, and Buffalo were major Underground Railroad cities with large free Black communities and active vigilance committees. From Buffalo, the crossing to Ontario was a short boat ride across the Niagara River. From Syracuse, the Oswego route led to Lake Ontario and the short crossing to Kingston or Toronto.

After Emancipation: Return and Continuity

After the Civil War and the ratification of the Thirteenth Amendment in 1865, some Black Canadians returned to the United States to participate in Reconstruction, to reunite with family members, or to take up new opportunities in a formally free nation. Others stayed in Canada, having built roots, families, and livelihoods that they were unwilling to abandon.

The communities they built — the churches, the schools, the newspapers, the mutual aid networks — did not disappear with emancipation. They became part of the long history of Black Canadian life, a legacy of the courage of freedom seekers, the organizing genius of Underground Railroad networks, and the communities, like Middletown's, that made the journey possible.

"The Geography of Resistance was not simply a map of escape routes. It was a map of communities — places where Black people, free and freedom-seeking, built the social infrastructure of liberation." — Paraphrased from Cheryl Janifer LaRoche, Free Black Communities and the Underground Railroad (2014)

What Freedom Meant

For those who arrived in Canada after weeks or months of travel — after hiding in barns, moving by night, crossing swollen rivers, and trusting strangers in strange cities — the meaning of freedom was immediate and physical. It meant waking up in the morning without fear of being sold. It meant children who would not be taken away. It meant the legal right to the wages of one's labor, to choose one's residence, to marry and have that marriage recognized by law.

It also meant grief. Families had often been separated. Some who reached freedom had left loved ones behind, unsure if they would ever see them again. Freedom was real and hard-won, but it existed in the shadow of everything that slavery had taken.

The story of the Underground Railroad, from Richmond to Middletown to Hamilton, is ultimately a story about what people are willing to risk and sacrifice for the right to be recognized as fully human. Middletown's Black community understood that. They acted on it. And the freedom seekers who passed through their care carried that understanding with them to the other side of the border, where they built lives worthy of everything the journey had cost.
