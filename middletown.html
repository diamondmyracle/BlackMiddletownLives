Middletown, Connecticut

A way station, a sanctuary, and a center of Black abolitionist life — how Middletown's churches, families, and reformers shaped the Underground Railroad.

Middletown as a "Way Station"

In his landmark study The Underground Railroad in Connecticut, Horatio T. Strother devoted an entire chapter to Middletown, describing it as a genuine "way station" — a term that captures both the practical and the communal dimensions of the city's role. A way station is more than a hiding place: it is an organized point of support where a traveler can rest, be provisioned, receive information, and be guided forward. Middletown offered all of this.

Situated on the west bank of the Connecticut River roughly twenty miles south of Hartford, Middletown was connected to the wider world by water and road. Ships and small boats moved along the river; stagecoach routes connected the city to New Haven to the south and Hartford to the north. This geographic position meant that freedom seekers arriving from coastal New England ports or overland from New York could be received and then moved efficiently northward toward Massachusetts and Vermont — and ultimately into Canada.

"Middletown had all the elements necessary for an effective station: a settled free Black community, trusted church networks, sympathetic white allies, and routes of exit in multiple directions." — Paraphrased from Horatio T. Strother, The Underground Railroad in Connecticut, Chapter 11

Middletown's Free Black Community

The Underground Railroad functioned precisely because free Black communities were willing to take extraordinary risks. In Middletown, a significant free Black population had taken root by the early nineteenth century. Black residents worked as sailors, domestic workers, artisans, and small business owners. They built institutions — churches, mutual aid societies, and schools — that formed the social infrastructure necessary for collective action.

Cheryl Janifer LaRoche's research emphasizes that free Black communities did not simply support the Underground Railroad: they were the Underground Railroad. The network depended on the everyday trust and cooperation of Black families who opened their homes, shared their food, and kept silence under pressure. In Middletown, these acts of solidarity were not isolated gestures — they were part of a sustained, organized commitment to freedom.

LaRoche further argues that the geography of free Black communities — where they settled, where their churches stood, where their social networks extended — directly shaped the routes and effectiveness of the Underground Railroad. Middletown's Black community, situated at a key river crossing and road junction, created a node in the network whose importance is difficult to overstate.

The Beman Family: Faith and Abolition

Among all the individuals connected to Middletown's Underground Railroad, none looms larger than Jehiel C. Beman (1798–1858), a Black minister, community leader, and tireless abolitionist. Born free in Connecticut, Beman became pastor of the African Methodist Episcopal Zion (AME Zion) church in Middletown and made it a center of antislavery activism.

Beman was an organizer and agitator of the first order. He participated in the national Negro Convention Movement — a series of meetings in which free Black leaders across the North developed strategies for fighting slavery and racial discrimination. He corresponded with prominent abolitionists, signed antislavery petitions, and used his pulpit to denounce the slave system and the Fugitive Slave laws. His church was not merely a place of worship; it was a command center for local abolitionist activity.

His son, Amos Gerry Beman (1812–1874), continued and amplified his father's legacy. Educated partly in Middletown and later at Wesleyan University's environs, Amos became a nationally recognized Black abolitionist and minister who served New Haven's Temple Street Church. The Beman family's multi-generational commitment to freedom exemplifies LaRoche's argument that family was one of the three pillars supporting the Underground Railroad movement.

Notable Abolitionists and Leaders Connected to Middletown

  • Jehiel C. Beman — AME Zion minister, Negro Convention Movement activist, organizer of antislavery efforts in Middletown
  • Amos Gerry Beman — Son of Jehiel; nationally known abolitionist, editor, and minister; educated in New Haven and active in fugitive slave defense cases
  • Local AME Zion congregation members — Unnamed but essential participants who housed, fed, and protected freedom seekers passing through the city
  • White abolitionist allies — Members of Middletown's antislavery society and sympathetic citizens who collaborated with Black community leaders

The Church as Fortress and Agent of Change

Donohue and Bayers, writing on AME and AME Zion churches in Connecticut, describe these institutions as simultaneously "fortresses of faith" — places of spiritual refuge and community identity — and "agents of change" — active participants in the social and political struggles of their time. In Middletown, the AME Zion church embodied both roles.

The church provided what no other institution could: a legitimate reason for Black people to gather, a communication network extending across state lines through visiting ministers and church correspondence, and moral authority that gave its members the courage to act even in the face of federal law. Ministers like Jehiel Beman moved between congregations throughout New England and New York, carrying information — and sometimes people — along with them.

LaRoche's "Ministers Chart" appendix documents the remarkable density of AME and AME Zion ministerial networks across the Northeast, showing how nearly every node of the Underground Railroad was connected to at least one Black church. In Connecticut, this meant that a freedom seeker arriving in New Haven could be passed to Middletown's congregation, sheltered, and then forwarded northward to Hartford and beyond, with each transition managed through trusted pastoral relationships.

"Family, church, and community were the three inseparable pillars supporting this geography of resistance." — Cheryl Janifer LaRoche, Free Black Communities and the Underground Railroad (2014), p. 127

Fugitive Slaves in Middletown

Documenting the specific individuals who passed through Middletown as freedom seekers is difficult — by design. The Underground Railroad operated in secrecy precisely because exposure meant recapture, imprisonment, or death. Names were changed, records were kept sparse or not at all, and those involved spoke carefully even in private correspondence. This archival silence is itself part of the historical record: it speaks to the danger that surrounded every act of assistance.

Nevertheless, Strother's research uncovers enough evidence to confirm that Middletown received freedom seekers and passed them onward. The city's free Black community and its abolitionist networks were capable of harboring individuals for days or weeks when necessary, and of arranging transport northward when conditions were safe. The Connecticut River, while a potential barrier in some seasons, also provided the possibility of river travel that avoided well-traveled roads where slave catchers might operate.

The Fugitive Slave Act of 1850 dramatically intensified the stakes. After its passage, even free Black people in northern cities faced the risk of kidnapping under claims of fugitive status. Middletown's Black community responded to this threat by intensifying its vigilance and its commitment to protecting those in danger — a response entirely consistent with Beman's ongoing abolitionist leadership.

The Path Through Middletown

Based on Strother's reconstruction and the broader geography of Connecticut's Underground Railroad, freedom seekers passing through Middletown most likely arrived from one of two directions: overland from New Haven to the southwest, or by river boat from coastal Long Island Sound ports. From Middletown, the route northward typically led toward Hartford — Connecticut's capital and another significant node in the Railroad — and then continued to Springfield, Massachusetts, a major hub connected to multiple onward routes into Vermont, New Hampshire, or New York State, and ultimately across the border into Canada.

Likely Route Through Connecticut

  1. New Haven, CT — coastal entry point with active Black community and abolitionist networks
  2. Middletown, CT — AME Zion church and Beman network provide shelter and re-provisioning
  3. Hartford, CT — larger city with additional church networks and sympathetic residents
  4. Springfield, MA — major junction connecting to multiple northward routes
  5. Vermont / upstate New York — final staging areas before crossing into Canada

Timeline of Key Moments

c. 1820s

Free Black community in Middletown grows; AME Zion congregation established.

1830s

Jehiel C. Beman active as pastor and abolitionist organizer; participates in the national Negro Convention Movement. Middletown's antislavery society founded with both Black and white members.

1838

Amos Gerry Beman attends Wesleyan University's preparatory school — a controversial act that highlights tensions over Black access to education in Connecticut.

1840s

Underground Railroad activity in Connecticut intensifies as northern antislavery sentiment grows. Middletown increasingly functions as an established way station.

1850

Fugitive Slave Act of 1850 passed. Danger to all Black people in northern cities escalates. Middletown's community responds with heightened vigilance and organizing.

1850s

Amos Gerry Beman deeply involved in fugitive slave defense cases in New Haven. The Beman network spans Connecticut and connects to national antislavery leadership.

1858

Jehiel C. Beman dies, but the community and networks he built endure.

1863

Emancipation Proclamation issued. The Underground Railroad's mission does not end — thousands still seek safety during the Civil War years.

Why Middletown Matters

The story of Middletown's Underground Railroad is ultimately a story about what organized community resistance looks like. It was not a single heroic act but a sustained collective commitment: ministers who used their churches as safe houses, families who shared their homes, ordinary citizens who maintained silence under pressure, and leaders like the Bemans who connected local efforts to a national movement for Black freedom.

Middletown's story also complicates narratives that center white abolitionists in the history of the Underground Railroad. Here, as in communities across the North, it was free Black people — building on their churches, their families, and their community institutions — who did the most dangerous and most essential work.
