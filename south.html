Southern Origins

The foundations of resistance — who sought freedom, and why the Underground Railroad emerged from the enslaved communities of the Upper South.

Slavery in the Upper South

The Underground Railroad did not emerge from a vacuum. It was born out of the daily suffering, determination, and ingenuity of enslaved people — men, women, and children — who refused to accept bondage as their permanent condition. By the early nineteenth century, the Upper South states of Virginia, Maryland, and Delaware had become the primary launching points for those who dared to flee northward.

Slavery in these states was entangled with every aspect of economic and social life. Enslaved people labored on tobacco and grain plantations, in domestic service, and in urban industries. Yet it was precisely this diversity of labor that gave some enslaved people the skills, mobility, and connections that made escape possible. Skilled tradespeople who traveled between towns, domestic workers who observed their enslavers' correspondence, and dockworkers who loaded ships all possessed knowledge that could be turned toward freedom.

"The act of running away itself was a powerful assertion of humanity — a refusal to be merely property." — Historian Cheryl Janifer LaRoche, Free Black Communities and the Underground Railroad (2014)

Who Sought Freedom?

Popular imagination often pictures the freedom seeker as a lone man fleeing under cover of darkness. The historical reality was far more varied. Women escaped carrying their children. Families planned together over months or years. Some freedom seekers were literate and wrote their own passes; others relied on conductors, free Black community members, and sympathetic white abolitionists who organized safe houses and guided routes.

The Fugitive Slave Act of 1793 — and its more powerful successor in 1850 — made the risks enormous. Enslavers hired professional slave catchers, posted rewards, and pressured northern communities to return those who had escaped. This legal machinery of recapture is essential context for understanding why northern cities like Middletown, Connecticut mattered: they were not simply stops on a route, but genuine sanctuaries embedded in organized Black communities capable of protecting, hiding, and advocating for those who arrived.

Building the Networks

Scholars including Cheryl Janifer LaRoche have emphasized that the Underground Railroad was not primarily a white abolitionist operation — it was built and sustained by free Black communities. Churches, fraternal organizations, mutual aid societies, and family networks formed the connective tissue of the Railroad. LaRoche argues that "family, church, and community" were the three inseparable pillars supporting this geography of resistance.

In southern states, free Black communities near port cities and along major roads served as the first links. AME and AME Zion congregations in Baltimore, Philadelphia, and other cities were gathering points where information was exchanged, resources were pooled, and courageous individuals like Harriet Tubman organized repeated missions southward to bring others out.

Key Conditions That Made Escape Possible

  • Proximity to free states and navigable rivers or roads heading north
  • Contact with free Black urban communities who passed information
  • Literacy or access to forged travel documents
  • AME and AME Zion church networks providing shelter and coordination
  • Sympathetic white Quakers, Methodists, and members of antislavery societies
  • Knowledge of the North Star and seasonal conditions for travel

The Role of African American Churches

Perhaps no institution was more central to the Underground Railroad than the Black church. As Donohue and Bayers explain in their study of AME and AME Zion churches in Connecticut, these congregations were simultaneously "fortresses of faith" and "agents of change." They provided not only spiritual sustenance but also practical resources: food, shelter, clothing, money, and — crucially — trusted networks of ministers and lay people stretching from the South into New England and Canada.

The AME Zion denomination in particular earned the nickname "the Freedom Church" because of its deep involvement in abolitionism and the Underground Railroad. Its ministers, educated and often widely traveled, acted as information brokers and organizers who could move people and resources across state lines under the cover of church business. This same tradition of institutional courage would manifest powerfully in Middletown, Connecticut.

From the South to Connecticut

The route northward from the Upper South typically passed through Philadelphia — a major hub with a large free Black population and active vigilance committees — then through New Jersey and New York City, and onward into New England. By the 1820s and 1830s, Connecticut's coastal towns and river cities had become important nodes in this network. The Connecticut River, flowing southward from Massachusetts, created both a natural corridor and a set of port communities that could receive, shelter, and pass along those fleeing slavery.

Middletown, situated on the Connecticut River approximately twenty miles south of Hartford, was ideally positioned to function as a way station: large enough to have an established free Black community, connected enough by river and road to receive arrivals from the south and dispatch them northward, yet small enough that trusted networks operated with discretion.
